Co-employment is the legal engine inside every PEO — and the word that scares owners until they see what it actually divides.

## How does co-employment split employer duties?

Cleanly, along the administrative line:

| The PEO takes | The owner keeps |
|---|---|
| Payroll and tax filings under its EIN | Hiring and firing decisions |
| Benefits sponsorship and administration | Wages, roles, and promotions |
| Workers' comp coverage and claims | Scheduling and daily management |
| Payroll-related compliance | Culture and customer relationships |

Employees notice almost nothing: same boss, same job, same desk — paychecks and W-2s simply arrive under the PEO's employer identification.

## What does co-employment unlock?

Scale. Because the PEO is the employer of record for thousands of small businesses at once, it buys benefits, workers' comp, and retirement options as one enormous group — and a nine-person company inside that pool prices like a big company. That pooling is the entire economic case for [what a PEO charges](/qa/how-much-does-a-peo-cost): the fee buys admission to purchasing power no small group reaches alone.

## What is co-employment not?

Not staffing, not leasing, not surrendering the team. The business's existing employees remain its employees in every operational sense; no third party supplies workers or directs them. Nor does it erase every obligation — worksite safety and lawful daily treatment of employees stay with the owner, and [unwinding the arrangement later](/qa/can-a-business-leave-a-peo) has real logistics. Where co-employment fits among the alternatives — payroll service, DIY, or full PEO — is mapped in [the total cost comparison](/blog-post/peo-vs-payroll-service-vs-diy).
