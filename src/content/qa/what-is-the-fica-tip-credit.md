This is the generous corner of tipped payroll — a federal credit that hands money back to food and beverage employers, and goes unclaimed constantly.

## How does the FICA tip credit work?

Employers pay the employer share of Social Security and Medicare — FICA — on all reported tips. The credit returns roughly that amount, for tips above a federal minimum wage threshold, as a dollar-for-dollar credit against federal income tax. The mechanics in sequence:

1. Employees report tips; the employer pays employer-side FICA on them.
2. Tips up to the federal minimum wage threshold are excluded from the calculation.
3. Employer FICA paid on tips above that line becomes the credit, claimed on the business tax return.

## Which businesses qualify?

Food and beverage employers where tipping is customary — the credit was written for restaurants and bars, and Las Vegas is full of them. It is a federal credit, so Nevada's refusal to allow a wage tip credit changes nothing here: a Strip-adjacent bar paying full state minimum wage still claims it. Two boundaries matter: the tips must be genuine tips (mandatory [service charges don't count](/qa/are-service-charges-taxed-like-tips)), and the workers must be serving food or beverages.

## Why does the credit go unclaimed?

Because it lives on the income tax return, not in the payroll system, and nobody connects the two. High-tip businesses can leave meaningful money on the table every year purely from not knowing.
