PEO pricing confuses owners because the invoice is bigger than a payroll service's — and the comparison is wrong from the start. Different products, different math.

## How do PEOs structure pricing?

Two models dominate:

| Model | How it works | Watch for |
|---|---|---|
| Percentage of payroll | Fee scales with total wages | Every raise raises the fee |
| Per-employee-per-month | Flat administrative fee per head | Cleaner to compare; check what's excluded |

Either way, the total runs meaningfully above a standalone payroll service — that much is by design, because the product is bigger.

## What does the fee actually buy?

The bundle: payroll and tax filings, HR support, compliance help, workers' compensation, benefits administration, and — the headline — access to benefits pricing pooled across the PEO's tens of thousands of worksite employees. The mechanism behind the pooling is [co-employment](/qa/what-is-co-employment), which lets a nine-person company buy benefits like a large one. A business already paying for a payroll service, an HR consultant, workers' comp, and a benefits program is often paying PEO-like money in pieces without noticing.

## How should a business compare the cost?

Against the full stack, not the payroll line: current vendor invoices, plus owner hours at their real value, plus the recruiting weight of better benefits, minus the PEO fee. Where the math lands depends heavily on headcount and whether benefits matter to the next hire — the complete comparison across DIY, payroll service, and PEO is worked through in [the total cost breakdown](/blog-post/peo-vs-payroll-service-vs-diy).
