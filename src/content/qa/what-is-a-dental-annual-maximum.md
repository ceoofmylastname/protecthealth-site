The annual maximum is the single most misunderstood number on a dental plan — mostly because it works backwards from every medical insurance instinct.

## How does a dental annual maximum work?

The annual maximum is the ceiling on what the plan pays per year, commonly $1,000 to $2,000. It is the mirror image of a medical out-of-pocket maximum:

| Feature | Medical out-of-pocket max | Dental annual max |
|---|---|---|
| What it caps | What the member pays | What the plan pays |
| After it's hit | Plan pays everything | Member pays everything |
| Typical range | Thousands | $1,000-$2,000 |

Medical insurance protects against catastrophe; dental insurance subsidizes maintenance and then steps aside. A bad dental year does not get safer as it gets worse — it gets more expensive.

## How fast does the maximum actually run out?

Faster than most people expect. A crown covered at 50% can put several hundred dollars of plan money on the board in one visit. Add the year's cleanings and a filling or two, and a $1,500 maximum can be exhausted by a single root-canal-and-crown sequence. This ceiling is exactly why the value of dental coverage lives in the routine layers — the premium-versus-usage arithmetic is laid out in [the three numbers that decide if dental insurance is worth it](/blog-post/is-dental-insurance-worth-it).

## How do people plan around the maximum?

Timing is the main lever. Dentists routinely phase large treatment plans across two plan years — one crown in December, the next in January — so each lands against a fresh maximum. The second lever is supplementing: dental discount plans have no maximums, so some households use insurance up to the cap and a discount membership for the overage. The third is plan selection itself: for a household with predictable major work ahead, a higher-maximum plan can be worth its higher premium.
