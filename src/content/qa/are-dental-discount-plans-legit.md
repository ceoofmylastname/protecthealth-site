The suspicion usually starts with the price — $10-ish a month for "dental savings" sounds like a catch. The product is real; the catch is only that it isn't what some ads imply it is.

## Why are discount plans legitimate?

Discount plans are an established membership model: dentists agree to a reduced fee schedule in exchange for patient volume, and members pay $80 to $200 a year to access those rates — commonly 10% to 60% off. The dentist gets paid in full at the discounted price, the member skips claims entirely, and reputable plans publish both the fee schedule and the dentist network before anyone pays a fee. Nothing in the structure requires trust in a future payout, which is exactly why it works.

## What is the catch, then?

The catch is categorical, not fraudulent: a discount plan is not insurance and is not regulated as insurance. That means:

1. **Nothing is ever covered** — every visit produces a bill; the bill is just smaller.
2. **No insurance-grade oversight** — no coverage mandates, no claims protections, lighter state registration at most.
3. **The network is the product** — a great fee schedule at dentists nobody can reach is worth nothing.

The full structural comparison against actual insurance — who wins where, and why — is in [the dental insurance versus discount plan breakdown](/blog-post/dental-insurance-vs-discount-plans).

## How should a discount plan be vetted?

Two checks do the whole job. First, pull the published fee schedule and price the procedures actually anticipated — a crown, a cleaning, a filling — against local cash rates; the discount should be visible in dollars, not adjectives. Second, call the specific dentist and confirm participation before joining, since networks change and directories lag. A plan that resists either check, or that markets itself as "coverage," has answered the legitimacy question already. Plans that pass both are legitimate tools — just tools of a different kind than insurance.
