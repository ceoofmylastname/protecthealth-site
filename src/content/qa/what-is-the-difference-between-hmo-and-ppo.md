The acronyms describe a trade: freedom versus price. Everything else about the HMO-versus-PPO decision is detail on that trade.

## What does each plan type actually do?

| | HMO | PPO |
|---|---|---|
| Out-of-network care | Not covered (except emergencies) | Covered at a reduced rate |
| Specialist access | Referral from primary care doctor | Direct, no referral |
| Premiums | Lower | Higher |
| Best for | Care concentrated in one local network | Flexibility, travel, specific specialists |

Two cousins fill out the family: EPOs (in-network only, but no referrals) and POS plans (referrals, but partial out-of-network coverage). The acronym is a starting point; the plan documents are the truth.

## Which type fits which household?

The deciding question is where care actually happens. A household whose doctors, hospital, and specialists all live inside one strong network gives up little by choosing the HMO and pockets the premium difference. A household with a must-keep specialist, regular travel, or care split across provider groups pays for PPO flexibility because losing access costs more. In Las Vegas, where provider-group contracts shift year to year, the network question deserves the verification steps in the [plan-selection framework](/blog-post/how-to-choose-a-health-insurance-plan-nevada).

## Why has genuine PPO access become a strategy question?

Because individual-market PPOs have grown scarce in many regions, including much of Nevada's marketplace, where HMO and EPO designs dominate the shelf. Households that assume "buying insurance" means "getting a PPO" often discover the difference at the specialist's front desk. Finding genuine PPO access — through the marketplace, employer coverage, or an ICHRA-funded individual plan — is exactly the kind of structural question a strategy conversation settles before enrollment, not after.
