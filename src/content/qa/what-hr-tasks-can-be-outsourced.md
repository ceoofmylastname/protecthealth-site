The useful question isn't whether HR can be outsourced — it's which pieces to hand off, in what order, without giving up the decisions that make the business the owner's.

## Which HR tasks outsource well?

Almost everything administrative, and roughly in this order of payoff:

1. **Payroll and tax filings** — the weekly grind with the sharpest penalties.
2. **Benefits administration** — enrollment, deductions, renewals, employee questions.
3. **Handbook and policy work** — documented policies instead of verbal folklore.
4. **Onboarding and records** — I-9s, W-4s, and files that survive an audit.
5. **Compliance monitoring** — deadlines, postings, and rule changes tracked by someone whose job it is.
6. **Workers' comp administration** — coverage, claims paperwork, and audits.

## What should stay in-house?

The judgment. Who gets hired, who gets promoted, who gets let go, how the team is run day to day — no provider can or should make those calls. The best arrangements put documentation and process behind the owner's decisions (a termination checklist, a compliant write-up template) while leaving the decision itself where it belongs. That division is why outsourced HR rarely feels like lost control in practice.

## How does a business choose the outsourcing model?

By matching the model to the burden. A five-person shop drowning only in payroll needs a payroll service; a fifteen-person business fielding compliance questions and benefits requests needs bundled HR support; some teams justify full PEO co-employment.
