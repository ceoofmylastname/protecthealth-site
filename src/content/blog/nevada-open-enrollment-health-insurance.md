Open enrollment is the one window each year when any Nevadan can buy individual health insurance, no questions asked. Miss the window, and the options shrink to a handful of special circumstances. This guide covers the dates, the money, and the mistakes.

## When does Nevada open enrollment start and end?

Nevada open enrollment runs **November 1 through January 15** through the [Silver State Health Insurance Exchange](/blog-post/silver-state-health-insurance-exchange), Nevada's state-based marketplace. Two timing rules matter:

- Enroll by **December 15** → coverage typically starts January 1
- Enroll December 16 through January 15 → coverage starts February 1

A plan selected on time but never paid does not activate. The first premium payment is what turns a selection into actual coverage.

## What if the window is missed?

A qualifying life event opens a **special enrollment period** — usually 60 days from the event. Qualifying events include losing job-based coverage, marriage or divorce, the birth or adoption of a child, and a permanent move into Nevada. Self-employed Nevadans losing group coverage from a former employer also qualify, which matters for new Realtors and 1099 contractors leaving W-2 jobs.

## What actually determines the right plan?

Premium and deductible are the two numbers everyone compares, and the two least useful in isolation. What "good coverage" looks like depends on:

| Factor | Why it changes the answer |
|--------|---------------------------|
| Household income | Determines premium tax-credit eligibility and cost-sharing reductions |
| Provider networks | The cheapest plan is expensive if preferred doctors are out of network |
| Prescriptions | Formularies differ; one medication can swing yearly costs by thousands |
| Expected usage | Frequent care favors richer plans; rare care favors lower premiums |
| Total exposure | Premium plus worst-case out-of-pocket max is the real annual price |

Two households with identical premiums can have completely different coverage quality. That is why ProtectHealth builds the strategy before naming a plan.

## What are the costly open-enrollment mistakes?

The three that cost Nevadans the most money: auto-renewing without rechecking tax credits (income changes silently change subsidies), buying on premium alone and discovering the network gap at the doctor's office, and guessing income far too low — which can mean paying tax credits back at filing time.
